\title{Support-Adaptive Specialist Merging for PFNs}

\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Prior-data fitted networks (PFNs) are pretrained on synthetic task priors, so deployment under prior shift is unavoidable. Broad synthetic prior mixtures are therefore attractive and often strong, but a single monolithic broad-prior PFN need not be the best finite model for every realized task. We propose a different design: first train a broad base PFN, then fine-tune a small set of specialist PFNs on structured generator families such as SCM-only, Tree-only, and NN-only data, and adapt only after observing the task support. The key theoretical object is not the soft specialist portfolio by itself, which is simply the hierarchical-Bayes reference, but the \emph{support-induced mixed-prior objective} that the realized task defines over those specialists. Support evidence produces a posterior over specialists, and that posterior in turn specifies a task-adapted mixed training surrogate. We prove that if specialist PFNs are fine-tuned from a shared broad base checkpoint and remain in a common locally quadratic basin, then support-weighted linear merging exactly minimizes that surrogate in the ideal case and remains stable under scorer error and local nonquadraticity, with suboptimality controlled by a quadratic penalty in weight mismatch plus a local remainder. A complementary learned-portfolio decomposition separates routing error, within-specialist approximation error, and merge compression. Exact Beta--Bernoulli calculations validate the broad-coverage picture and motivate the learned benchmark. The resulting design rule is simple: pretrain broadly, specialize by generator family, infer a support posterior, and if compute is tight merge the relevant specialists back into one task-adapted PFN.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Prior-data fitted networks (PFNs) learn predictive rules by pretraining on synthetic tasks rather than by fitting one dataset at a time \citep{muller2022,nagler2023}. Once PFNs are used as tabular foundation models, the central modeling question becomes a prior-design question: what synthetic tasks should be seen during pretraining, and how should they be organized? Broad synthetic mixtures, shift-aware priors, and expert-style variants have all been proposed \citep{helli2024,zhang2025,xu2025mixturepfn,grinsztajn2025tabpfn25,qu2026tabiclv2}, and a broad library is often the right hedge against the fact that the deployment prior is unknown at training time.

But broad is not the same thing as monolithic. A single PFN trained on a very broad library can be an excellent hedge and still fail to be the best finite-capacity approximation for each realized task. That gap is especially natural when the broad library itself is heterogeneous, for example mixing structural-causal, tree-based, and neural-network-style generators. In that setting, a single broad PFN must approximate several different posterior mechanisms at once, while a few generator-specific specialists may each be easier to fit accurately.

This suggests a two-stage design. First pretrain a broad base PFN on the full synthetic library. Then fine-tune a small number of specialists, for example an SCM-only specialist, a Tree-only specialist, and an NN-only specialist, all starting from the same base checkpoint. At deployment time, do not ask which prior should have been chosen in advance. Instead, use the observed support data to decide how much each specialist should contribute to the realized task.

This leads to a much cleaner object than ex ante prior partitioning. For a realized support set $D_n$, every specialist PFN induces a support evidence score: its prior weight plus its cumulative prequential log loss on the support. These evidence scores can be used in three progressively cheaper ways:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=18pt]
\item \textbf{Soft aggregation}: weight all specialists by posterior evidence.
\item \textbf{Hard selection}: choose the single best specialist under that posterior.
\item \textbf{Support-adaptive merge}: use the same posterior weights to synthesize several specialists into one task-adapted checkpoint.
\end{enumerate}

The first contribution of this paper is to identify the right theoretical object after the support has been observed. Conditioning on a latent specialist index is standard hierarchical Bayes, but in PFNs it has an additional consequence: the resulting support posterior does not only rank specialists, it induces a \emph{task-specific mixed-prior training objective} over synthetic generator families. That induced objective is the bridge between prior design and deployment-time model synthesis.

The second contribution is to explain where model merging belongs in this story. Model merging is \emph{not} the exact mixed-prior Bayes predictor in general; the latter uses support-dependent output weights. But if the specialist PFNs are fine-tuned from a shared broad base checkpoint and remain in a common locally quadratic basin, then support-weighted linear merging approximately solves the support-induced mixed training objective, with a clean stability bound in the scorer mismatch and the deviation from that common basin. When the local curvatures differ, Fisher-weighted merging is the principled second-order correction. This connects PFN prior design directly to model soups \citep{wortsman2022}, task arithmetic \citep{ilharco2022task}, Fisher-weighted averaging \citep{matena2022fisher}, and re-basin arguments for mergeability \citep{entezari2022,ainsworth2022git}.

The message is deliberately different from ``guess the right prior family.'' Broad pretraining remains important, but it should produce a strong base model rather than the final deployment answer. Then train a small specialist basis. Rank specialists by support evidence. Use soft aggregation as the exact hierarchical-Bayes reference. Then decide how much compute you can spend: keep the full soft portfolio, truncate to top-$k$, hard-select one model, or merge several specialists back into a single task-adapted checkpoint of unchanged size. In short, prior design becomes a broad-to-specialist training pipeline followed by post-support adaptation.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=18pt]
\item We formalize a broad-base-plus-specialists view of PFN prior design and identify support evidence as the posterior over generator specialists; pairwise evidence gaps are posterior log-odds whose expectation accumulates sequential KL separation.
\item We show that this support posterior induces a task-specific mixed-prior training objective, and we prove that support-adaptive linear merging is exact in a common quadratic basin and otherwise incurs only a quadratic penalty in weight mismatch plus a local nonquadratic remainder.
\item We give an approximation hierarchy for learned support-adaptive PFNs: routing error, within-specialist approximation error, and merge compression; Fisher merging and weighted task vectors appear as second- and first-order refinements.
\item We provide exact Beta--Bernoulli validations of broad coverage and support-adaptive deployment, and we specify the learned benchmark in which a broad PFN is specialized into SCM-only, Tree-only, and NN-only variants.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Our starting point is the PFN view that pretraining learns a Bayes posterior predictive under a synthetic task prior \citep{muller2022,nagler2023,muller2025position}. This explains why PFNs can be strong small-data predictors \citep{hollmann2025}, but it does not by itself answer how one should organize multiple priors when the deployment family is unknown.

One line of work strengthens a \emph{single} PFN by changing the synthetic library itself. Drift-Resilient TabPFN builds a temporal-shift prior on top of SCM generators \citep{helli2024}. MITRA studies which synthetic priors should be mixed and trains one stronger model on that curated mixture \citep{zhang2025}. TabPFN-2.5 and TabICLv2 continue this trend toward broader generators, larger-scale pretraining, and stronger single-model deployment \citep{grinsztajn2025tabpfn25,qu2026tabiclv2}. Our paper starts from the same premise that broad coverage is valuable, but draws a different conclusion: a broad library should produce a strong \emph{base} PFN, after which deployment should adapt across a small basis of generator specialists rather than commit to one monolithic broad model.

The closest systems paper to ours is MixturePFN \citep{xu2025mixturepfn}, which brings mixture-of-experts style routing \citep{jacobs1991} to tabular PFNs. MixturePFN clusters the realized training dataset, builds a pool of prompters/experts tied to those clusters, and routes test points to the selected expert at inference; its context-aware fine-tuning further specializes experts to the realized dataset via adapters. That is a different operating point from ours. MixturePFN is a routed multi-expert serving system whose active expert pool depends on the realized dataset and remains explicit at inference. By contrast, we keep a \emph{small fixed} set of specialists defined by generator families such as SCM-only, Tree-only, and NN-only, infer a single support posterior over those specialists once per task, and optionally compress that posterior into one fixed-size checkpoint by model merging. In short, MixturePFN is closest to pointwise routing over dataset clusters, whereas our object is task-level support adaptation and compression over prior specialists.

There is also a practical ecosystem around post-hoc combinations of TabPFN models. Prior Labs' AutoTabPFN tooling already exposes post-hoc ensembles over multiple TabPFN variants, but the public extension is documented and implemented as a random search over the TabPFN hyperparameter space followed by AutoGluon weighting, rather than as an explicit switch over synthetic prior families \citep{priorlabs2025ensembles}. TabPFN-2.5 then introduces a distillation engine that compresses a strong PFN into a fast MLP or tree ensemble for deployment \citep{grinsztajn2025tabpfn25}. These are complementary rather than competing with our goal. Post-hoc ensembles show that simply running several PFNs is already useful in practice, so our novelty is not ``multiple PFNs'' by itself. Distillation, in turn, is a downstream compression step for a chosen PFN predictor. Our contribution lives one level earlier: how to construct a stronger support-adaptive PFN object in the first place, before any optional serving-time distillation.

At the statistical level, hierarchical Bayes and log-loss aggregation provide the reference identities behind our support posterior \citep{hoeting1999,feder2021}. We use those facts only as the starting point. Our theoretical claim begins one step later: for PFNs, the support posterior over prior-specialist models induces a task-specific mixed training objective, and model merging can approximately solve that induced objective when specialists share a broad-base basin.

Finally, our model-merge section connects PFN prior specialization to the modern model-merging literature. Model soups justify weight averaging inside a common low-error basin \citep{wortsman2022}; task arithmetic interprets fine-tuning deltas as approximately additive task vectors \citep{ilharco2022task}; Fisher-weighted averaging gives a second-order merge rule \citep{matena2022fisher}; and re-basin work clarifies when independent models can be aligned well enough to merge \citep{entezari2022,ainsworth2022git}. We use those ideas not as standalone engineering tricks, but as a theory of when a support-adapted PFN portfolio can be compressed into one checkpoint.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

Let $\cT$ denote a task space. Each task $t\in\cT$ induces a distribution $P_t$ on one observation $Z=(X,Y)\in\cZ$. A task prior $\pi$ generates an episode by drawing
\[
t\sim \pi,\qquad Z_1,\dots,Z_n,Z_{n+1}\stackrel{i.i.d.}{\sim} P_t.
\]
We write $D_n:=Z_{1:n}$.

For any prior $\pi$, the induced $n$-sample mixture law is
\[
m_\pi^n(d_n)
:=
\int_{\cT}\prod_{i=1}^n P_t(z_i)\,\pi(\dd t),
\]
and the corresponding oracle posterior predictive is
\[
q_n^\pi(z_{n+1}\mid d_n)
:=
\frac{m_\pi^{n+1}(d_n,z_{n+1})}{m_\pi^n(d_n)}.
\]

Now fix a finite portfolio of specialist priors $\pi_1,\dots,\pi_M$ and a prior over specialists $\rho\in\Delta_M$. Let $F\in\{1,\dots,M\}$ denote the latent specialist index. The hierarchical prior first draws
\[
F\sim \rho,
\qquad
t\mid F=j \sim \pi_j,
\]
then generates the episode from $P_t$.

The practical case we have in mind is a broad generator library together with a few fine-grained subfamilies. For example, $\pi_1,\pi_2,\pi_3$ may correspond to SCM-style, tree-style, and neural-network-style synthetic generators, while a separate broad base prior mixes all three. The theory below treats the specialists abstractly, but Section~\ref{sec:learned} returns to this concrete broad-base-plus-specialists picture.

For each specialist $j$, define
\[
m_j^n:=m_{\pi_j}^n,
\qquad
q_{j,n}:=q_n^{\pi_j}.
\]
The hierarchical mixture law is
\[
m_\rho^n(d_n):=\sum_{j=1}^M \rho_j m_j^n(d_n),
\]
and its oracle posterior predictive is
\[
q_{\rho,n}(z_{n+1}\mid d_n):=\frac{m_\rho^{n+1}(d_n,z_{n+1})}{m_\rho^n(d_n)}.
\]

The support evidence posterior over specialists is
\[
w_j(d_n)
:=
\Pbb(F=j\mid D_n=d_n)
=
\frac{\rho_j m_j^n(d_n)}{\sum_{k=1}^M \rho_k m_k^n(d_n)}.
\]
Since
\[
m_j^n(d_n)=\prod_{i=1}^n q_{j,i-1}(z_i\mid d_{i-1}),
\]
the log evidence score can be written as
\[
S_j(d_n)
:=
\log \rho_j + \log m_j^n(d_n)
=
\log \rho_j - \sum_{i=1}^n \bigl[-\log q_{j,i-1}(z_i\mid d_{i-1})\bigr].
\]
So ranking specialists by posterior evidence is exactly the same as ranking them by prior weight plus cumulative support log loss.

\section{From Broad Base to Specialist Weights}
\label{sec:portfolio}

This section isolates the middle part of the paper's story. Broad synthetic pretraining is still the right hedge against unknown deployment, but a monolithic broad PFN need not be the best finite-capacity model for the realized task. The alternative is to start from a broad base model, specialize it into a few generator-specific PFNs, and use the support set to determine their weights.

Figure~\ref{fig:global-hedge} captures the paper's starting point. Broad synthetic pretraining is the right coverage decision when deployment is unknown. But it is only a coverage decision. Even though none of the plotted deployment priors coincides exactly with a training prior, the broad mixture remains the robust default, while each fixed deployment still has a better matched specialist. This is exactly the regime where a broad base PFN plus support-adapted specialists becomes attractive.

\subsection{Support Evidence Defines the Reference Object}

For exact specialists, conditioning on the latent specialist index immediately gives the hierarchical reference predictor
\[
q_{\rho,n}(\cdot\mid d_n)
=
\sum_{j=1}^M w_j(d_n)\, q_{j,n}(\cdot\mid d_n),
\qquad
w_j(d_n)\propto \rho_j m_j^n(d_n).
\]
Equivalently, the cumulative support log loss of that soft portfolio satisfies
\[
-\log m_\rho^n(d_n)
\le
\min_j\Bigl\{-\log \rho_j - \log m_j^n(d_n)\Bigr\}.
\]
These are the correct reference identities, but we do not treat them as the main novelty of the paper. They simply specify what any practical support-adaptive PFN should try to approximate once the support has been observed.

\paragraph{Support evidence is the right statistic.}
The score
\[
S_j(D_n)=\log \rho_j-\sum_{i=1}^n[-\log q_{j,i-1}(z_i\mid d_{i-1})]
\]
is not an ad hoc validation metric. Appendix~\ref{app:soft-and-evidence} shows that pairwise score differences are exactly posterior log-odds between specialists, and that under a well-specified specialist their expectation grows by cumulative sequential KL discrimination against competing specialists. This is why ranking SCM, Tree, and NN specialists by support performance is statistically well founded.

\paragraph{Learned specialist portfolios.}
The oracle reference above is only the first layer. In a learned system, each oracle specialist $q_{j,n}$ is replaced by a learned PFN $\widehat q_{j,n}$ and the oracle posterior weights $w_j(D_n)$ are replaced by a computable support scorer $\widehat w_j(D_n)$. Appendix~\ref{app:proof-approx} shows that
\[
\KL\bigl(q_{\rho,n}(\cdot\mid d_n)\,\|\,\widehat q_n(\cdot\mid d_n)\bigr)
\le
\KL\bigl(w(d_n)\,\|\,\widehat w(d_n)\bigr)
+
\sum_{j=1}^M w_j(d_n)\,
\KL\bigl(q_{j,n}(\cdot\mid d_n)\,\|\,\widehat q_{j,n}(\cdot\mid d_n)\bigr),
\]
so the learned portfolio gap already separates into routing error and within-specialist approximation error. This decomposition is more important for the paper than the soft-portfolio identity itself, because it tells us what remains to be controlled before any deployment-time merge is attempted.

\section{Practical Deployment at Fixed Model Size}

Once specialist weights are available, deployment reduces to a compute choice. One can keep the oracle reference in output space via soft aggregation, approximate it by hard or top-$k$ routing, or synthesize one support-adapted model of unchanged size by merging specialist updates.

\subsection{Hard Selection and Top-\texorpdfstring{$k$}{k} Truncation}

Soft aggregation is the exact reference predictor, but it can be expensive at inference time because every specialist must be evaluated. Two practical compressions are hard selection and top-$k$ truncation.

Let
\[
j^\star(d_n):=\arg\max_j w_j(d_n)
\]
be the posterior-best specialist and let
\[
q_{\hard,n}(\cdot\mid d_n):=q_{j^\star(d_n),n}(\cdot\mid d_n).
\]
For a set $K(d_n)\subseteq \{1,\dots,M\}$, let
\[
r_K(d_n):=\sum_{j\in K(d_n)} w_j(d_n),
\]
and define the renormalized top-$k$ predictor
\[
q_{\topk,n}(\cdot\mid d_n)
:=
\sum_{j\in K(d_n)} \frac{w_j(d_n)}{r_K(d_n)}\, q_{j,n}(\cdot\mid d_n).
\]
These choices admit immediate one-step penalty bounds:
\[
-\log q_{\hard,n}(z\mid d_n)
-
\bigl[-\log q_{\rho,n}(z\mid d_n)\bigr]
\le
-\log w_{j^\star(d_n)}(d_n),
\]
\[
-\log q_{\topk,n}(z\mid d_n)
-
\bigl[-\log q_{\rho,n}(z\mid d_n)\bigr]
\le
-\log r_K(d_n).
\]
If $w_{(1)}(d_n)\ge w_{(2)}(d_n)\ge \cdots \ge w_{(M)}(d_n)$ and
\[
\gamma(d_n):=\log \frac{w_{(1)}(d_n)}{w_{(2)}(d_n)},
\]
then
\[
w_{(1)}(d_n)\ge \frac{1}{1+(M-1)e^{-\gamma(d_n)}},
\]
so the hard-selection penalty is at most $\log(1+(M-1)e^{-\gamma(d_n)})$. The proofs are one-line inequalities and are deferred to Appendix~\ref{app:compression-lemmas}. These bounds are useful, but they are not the theoretical centerpiece of the paper; they simply quantify the compute trade-off once the specialist posterior has already been formed. In exact Beta--Bernoulli calculations, soft aggregation matches the monolithic hierarchical-prior oracle, top-$2$ tracks it closely, and hard selection is looser but still substantially better than committing to one fixed specialist in advance.

Figure~\ref{fig:weighted-vs-onehot} is the missing bridge from broad coverage to merge. Even in the exact oracle toy, there is no universally best interpolation coefficient between the two specialists $\operatorname{Beta}(8,2)$ and $\operatorname{Beta}(3,6)$. The deployment-optimal $\alpha^\star$ moves with the realized task. This already rules out both fixed uniform hedging and fixed one-hot commitment as universal deployment rules. The point of support adaptation is therefore to estimate a task-specific interpolation coefficient from the observed support. Hard selection is the discrete extreme of that idea; merge is the continuous fixed-size implementation.

\subsection{Support-Adaptive Merging from a Shared Base}
\label{sec:merge}

The previous section compressed the soft portfolio in output space. Model merging performs the same adaptation in parameter space by synthesizing one deployment checkpoint from a shared base model and a few specialist deltas.

\paragraph{Mixed-prior training objective.}

Let $\theta$ denote PFN parameters and let
\[
L_{\pi}(\theta)
:=
\E_{E\sim \pi}\bigl[\ell(\theta;E)\bigr]
\]
denote the pretraining objective under synthetic prior $\pi$, where $E$ is a sampled episode. For a mixture prior
\[
\pi_\alpha := \sum_{j=1}^M \alpha_j \pi_j,
\]
linearity of expectation gives the exact identity
\[
L_{\pi_\alpha}(\theta)=\sum_{j=1}^M \alpha_j L_{\pi_j}(\theta).
\]
So mixed-prior training is literally a weighted sum of the specialist training objectives.

This creates a natural bridge from support evidence to model merging. Once support data produce posterior weights $w(d_n)$, one can view them as defining a \emph{task-adapted mixed training surrogate}
\[
L_{w(d_n)}(\theta):=\sum_{j=1}^M w_j(d_n)\,L_{\pi_j}(\theta).
\]
The question becomes: when does merging the specialist checkpoints approximate the minimizer of this surrogate?

In the broad-base picture, let $\theta_0$ denote a PFN pretrained on the full heterogeneous synthetic library and let each specialist be obtained by fine-tuning that base:
\[
\theta_j = \theta_0 + \Delta_j.
\]
Given support data $d_n$, the deployment-time merged model is then
\[
\theta_{\mathrm{merge}}(d_n)
:=
\theta_0 + \sum_{j=1}^M \alpha_j(d_n)\Delta_j,
\]
where ideally $\alpha(d_n)=w(d_n)$ and in practice $\alpha(d_n)$ is computed from learned support evidence scores. The role of the next result is to justify this synthesis step.

\begin{theorem}[Support-adaptive merge solves the induced mixed objective]
\label{thm:merge-stability}
Fix support $d_n$ and let $w:=w(d_n)\in\Delta_M$ be the resulting specialist posterior. For any coefficients $\alpha\in\Delta_M$, define the linear merge
\[
\theta_\alpha(d_n):=\theta_0+\sum_{j=1}^M \alpha_j(\theta_j-\theta_0)=\sum_{j=1}^M \alpha_j\theta_j.
\]
Assume there is a neighborhood $U$, a positive-definite matrix $H$, and a scalar $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{quad}}\ge 0$ such that for every specialist $j$ and every $\theta\in U$,
\[
L_{\pi_j}(\theta)
=
c_j + \frac{1}{2}(\theta-\theta_j)^\top H(\theta-\theta_j)+r_j(\theta),
\qquad
|r_j(\theta)|\le \varepsilon_{\mathrm{quad}}.
\]
Assume also that $\|\theta_j-\theta_0\|\le R$ for every $j$, and let
\[
\theta_w^\star(d_n)\in \arg\min_{\theta\in U} \sum_{j=1}^M w_j(d_n)L_{\pi_j}(\theta).
\]
Then
\[
L_{w(d_n)}\bigl(\theta_\alpha(d_n)\bigr)-L_{w(d_n)}\bigl(\theta_w^\star(d_n)\bigr)
\le
\frac{\lambda_{\max}(H)}{2}R^2\|\alpha-w(d_n)\|_1^2
+
2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{quad}},
\]
where $L_{w(d_n)}(\theta):=\sum_{j=1}^M w_j(d_n)L_{\pi_j}(\theta)$. In particular, if $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{quad}}=0$ and $\alpha=w(d_n)$, then support-weighted linear merging is an exact minimizer of the support-adapted mixed objective.
\end{theorem}
\noindent\emph{Proof deferred to Appendix~\ref{app:proof-merge}.}

Theorem~\ref{thm:merge-stability} is the actual theoretical core of the paper. Support evidence is not only a model-selection score; it turns the realized task into a task-specific mixed-prior \emph{training objective}. Model merging is justified precisely when it approximately solves that induced objective. The theorem makes the two approximation sources explicit: scorer error enters through $\|\alpha-w(d_n)\|_1^2$, and geometry error enters through the local nonquadratic remainder $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{quad}}$.

Combined with the learned-portfolio decomposition from Section~\ref{sec:portfolio}, this yields the paper's approximation hierarchy:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=18pt]
\item routing error in forming the specialist posterior,
\item within-specialist approximation error in the learned PFNs,
\item merge compression error from replacing the support-induced mixed objective by one checkpoint.
\end{enumerate}
That three-way split, rather than the soft-portfolio identity alone, is what explains why broad-base pretraining plus specialist merging is a coherent design principle.

Appendix~\ref{app:proof-fisher} shows that when the local curvatures differ, the corresponding second-order refinement is Fisher/Hessian-weighted merging rather than plain averaging. Appendix~\ref{app:proof-task} further shows that there is a first-order task-vector interpretation. With $\alpha=w(d_n)$,
\[
\sum_j w_j(d_n)\Delta_j
\approx
-\eta P \nabla \left(\sum_j w_j(d_n)L_{\pi_j}\right)(\theta_0),
\]
so the merged task vector approximates the gradient step for the support-adapted mixed objective. This makes the connection to task arithmetic \citep{ilharco2022task} direct.

Model merging still does not implement hierarchical Bayes exactly. The oracle acts in output space through support-dependent predictive averaging, whereas merging acts in parameter space through a support-induced surrogate objective. The point is not that merging \emph{is} the Bayes solution, but that it is the right fixed-size approximation once the support posterior has been formed.

\section{Learned-PFN Benchmark Implied by the Theory}
\label{sec:learned}

The theoretical story suggests a concrete learned benchmark with one base PFN and a small set of specialists.

\paragraph{Training.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=18pt]
\item Train a broad base PFN on a heterogeneous synthetic library that mixes several generator classes.
\item Fine-tune specialist PFNs from that same checkpoint on a small number of structured generator families, for example SCM-only, Tree-only, and NN-only data.
\item Keep the original broad base PFN and, optionally, separately train one monolithic PFN on the explicit hierarchical mixture of those same families as a reference.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Test-time methods.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=18pt]
\item Broad base PFN with no support adaptation.
\item Monolithic hierarchical PFN.
\item Soft evidence-weighted portfolio.
\item Hard selection.
\item Top-$k$ sparse soft portfolio.
\item Linear support-adaptive merge
\[
\theta_0+\alpha_{\mathrm{SCM}}(D_n)\Delta_{\mathrm{SCM}}
+\alpha_{\mathrm{Tree}}(D_n)\Delta_{\mathrm{Tree}}
+\alpha_{\mathrm{NN}}(D_n)\Delta_{\mathrm{NN}}.
\]
\item Fisher-weighted merge with the same support-dependent coefficients.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=18pt]
\item Query log loss, Brier score, and calibration after observing a support set of size $n$.
\item Support evidence concentration curves $w_{(1)}(D_n)$ and retained top-$k$ mass.
\item Whether merged specialists beat the unchanged broad base model at the same deployment model size.
\item Distance between merged checkpoints and the directly mixed-prior-trained PFN, measured in parameter space and predictive space.
\item Interpolation barriers and, if needed, re-basin ablations to test the shared-basin assumption.
\end{enumerate}

This benchmark cleanly separates the three theoretical layers of the paper:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=18pt]
\item \textbf{Oracle layer}: soft evidence weighting.
\item \textbf{Output-compression layer}: hard and top-$k$ truncation.
\item \textbf{Parameter-compression layer}: linear and Fisher merging.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}

The story that emerges is simple.

\paragraph{1. Prior design should start broad, then specialize.}
When the deployment family is unknown, broad synthetic pretraining remains the correct hedge. But the broad model should be treated as a base representation, not as the only deployment model we are allowed to use.

\paragraph{2. Support evidence is the natural adaptation signal.}
The realized support set already contains exactly the statistic needed to rank specialists: prior weight plus cumulative prequential log loss. More importantly, that same posterior over specialists induces the task-specific mixed-prior objective that the deployment model should solve. No extra router target has to be invented.

\paragraph{3. Soft portfolios are the reference; merge is the real synthesis theorem.}
Soft evidence weighting is exact hierarchical Bayes, but only as a reference object. The paper's real theoretical claim is that support evidence defines a mixed-prior training surrogate, and that shared-base specialists can be merged to approximately solve that surrogate while keeping deployment model size fixed.

\paragraph{4. Model merging belongs in the paper, but not as the main oracle object.}
The correct theoretical role of model merging is not ``this is the Bayes solution.'' It is ``this is when support evidence turns prior mismatch into a solvable mixed-objective approximation problem.'' That statement is stronger than simple checkpoint compression, and it is precisely where linear mode connectivity, task vectors, and Fisher-weighted averaging become relevant.

\paragraph{5. The proposal is upstream of later deployment compression.}
Our goal is to strengthen the PFN itself under prior mismatch by moving from one monolithic broad model to a support-adaptive specialist basis. That is different from downstream accelerators such as post-hoc ensembles or TabPFN-2.5's distillation engine. In fact the two layers are compatible: once a stronger support-adaptive PFN or merged PFN has been constructed, standard distillation machinery could be applied afterwards if a cheaper MLP or tree ensemble is needed in production.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper proposes a different answer to the PFN prior-design problem. Instead of asking for one synthetic prior library that is correct before the task is seen, we propose to pretrain broadly, fine-tune a small set of generator specialists, and adapt only after the task support is observed. The soft specialist portfolio is the correct hierarchical-Bayes reference, but the paper's theoretical core is one step later: the support posterior induces a task-specific mixed-prior training objective, and under shared-base geometry a merged checkpoint approximately solves that objective with an explicit stability bound. Hard selection and top-$k$ remain useful output-space approximations, but model merging is what turns post-support adaptation into a fixed-size deployment rule. The resulting design rule is practical and asymmetric: hedge broadly during pretraining, specialize into a small basis, and merge those specialists back together only after the support reveals what kind of task has arrived.

\section{Support Evidence and Portfolio Bounds}
\label{app:soft-and-evidence}

\begin{lemma}[Soft aggregation identity]
\label{lem:soft-identity}
For every context length $n$ and every realized support $d_n$,
\[
q_{\rho,n}(\cdot\mid d_n)
=
\sum_{j=1}^M w_j(d_n)\, q_{j,n}(\cdot\mid d_n).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Use
\[
m_\rho^{n+1}(d_n,z)=\sum_{j=1}^M \rho_j m_j^{n+1}(d_n,z)
=
\sum_{j=1}^M \rho_j m_j^n(d_n) q_{j,n}(z\mid d_n),
\]
then divide by $m_\rho^n(d_n)=\sum_j \rho_j m_j^n(d_n)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Best-specialist support bound]
\label{lem:best-specialist}
For every realized support $d_n$,
\[
-\log m_\rho^n(d_n)
\le
\min_j\Bigl\{-\log \rho_j - \log m_j^n(d_n)\Bigr\}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Since $m_\rho^n(d_n)=\sum_j \rho_j m_j^n(d_n)\ge \rho_j m_j^n(d_n)$ for every $j$, the claim follows by taking logs and using the prequential factorization of each $m_j^n$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Support evidence equals posterior log-odds]
\label{prop:evidence-odds}
Fix two specialists $j$ and $k$. For every realized support $d_n$,
\[
\log \frac{w_j(d_n)}{w_k(d_n)}
=
\log \frac{\rho_j}{\rho_k}
+
\sum_{i=1}^n
\log \frac{q_{j,i-1}(z_i\mid d_{i-1})}{q_{k,i-1}(z_i\mid d_{i-1})}.
\]
If the episode is generated from specialist $j$, that is $D_n\sim m_j^n$, then
\[
\E_{D_n\sim m_j^n}
\left[
\log \frac{w_j(D_n)}{w_k(D_n)}
\right]
=
\log \frac{\rho_j}{\rho_k}
+
\sum_{i=1}^n
\E_{D_{i-1}\sim m_j^{i-1}}
\KL\bigl(
q_{j,i-1}(\cdot\mid D_{i-1})
\,
\|
\,
q_{k,i-1}(\cdot\mid D_{i-1})
\bigr).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The first identity is immediate from
\[
\frac{w_j(d_n)}{w_k(d_n)}
=
\frac{\rho_j m_j^n(d_n)}{\rho_k m_k^n(d_n)}
\]
and the prequential factorization of each $m_j^n$. For the expectation, expand the same factorization and use the tower property:
\[
\E_{D_n\sim m_j^n}\log \frac{m_j^n(D_n)}{m_k^n(D_n)}
=
\sum_{i=1}^n
\E_{D_{i-1}\sim m_j^{i-1}}
\E_{Z_i\sim q_{j,i-1}(\cdot\mid D_{i-1})}
\left[
\log \frac{q_{j,i-1}(Z_i\mid D_{i-1})}{q_{k,i-1}(Z_i\mid D_{i-1})}
\right].
\]
The inner expectation is exactly the displayed KL term.
\end{proof}

\section{Learned-Portfolio Decomposition}
\label{app:proof-approx}

\begin{theorem}[Mixture decomposition for learned portfolios]
\label{thm:approx-portfolio}
For every realized support $d_n$,
\[
\KL\bigl(q_{\rho,n}(\cdot\mid d_n)\,\|\,\widehat q_n(\cdot\mid d_n)\bigr)
\le
\KL\bigl(w(d_n)\,\|\,\widehat w(d_n)\bigr)
+
\sum_{j=1}^M w_j(d_n)\,
\KL\bigl(q_{j,n}(\cdot\mid d_n)\,\|\,\widehat q_{j,n}(\cdot\mid d_n)\bigr).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Consider the joint laws on $(F,Z_{n+1})$ with shared conditional support $d_n$:
\[
P(F=j,Z_{n+1}\in \dd z)=w_j(d_n)\,q_{j,n}(z\mid d_n)\,\dd z,
\]
\[
Q(F=j,Z_{n+1}\in \dd z)=\widehat w_j(d_n)\,\widehat q_{j,n}(z\mid d_n)\,\dd z.
\]
By the chain rule for KL,
\[
\KL(P\|Q)
=
\KL(w\|\widehat w)
+
\sum_j w_j \KL(q_j\|\widehat q_j).
\]
Marginalizing out $F$ sends $(P,Q)$ to $(q_{\rho,n},\widehat q_n)$, so data processing gives
\[
\KL(q_{\rho,n}\|\widehat q_n)\le \KL(P\|Q).
\]
\end{proof}

\section{Compression Lemmas for Hard and Top-\texorpdfstring{$k$}{k}}
\label{app:compression-lemmas}

\begin{lemma}[Compression penalties]
\label{lem:compression}
For every support $d_n$ and every next outcome $z$,
\[
-\log q_{\hard,n}(z\mid d_n)
-
\bigl[-\log q_{\rho,n}(z\mid d_n)\bigr]
\le
-\log w_{j^\star(d_n)}(d_n),
\]
and
\[
-\log q_{\topk,n}(z\mid d_n)
-
\bigl[-\log q_{\rho,n}(z\mid d_n)\bigr]
\le
-\log r_K(d_n).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Since
\[
q_{\rho,n}(z\mid d_n)
=
\sum_j w_j(d_n) q_{j,n}(z\mid d_n)
\ge
w_{j^\star(d_n)}(d_n)\,q_{\hard,n}(z\mid d_n),
\]
the first claim follows after taking logs. Likewise,
\[
q_{\rho,n}(z\mid d_n)
\ge
\sum_{j\in K(d_n)} w_j(d_n) q_{j,n}(z\mid d_n)
=
r_K(d_n)\,q_{\topk,n}(z\mid d_n).
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Evidence-gap control]
\label{lem:evidence-gap}
Let $w_{(1)}(d_n)\ge w_{(2)}(d_n)\ge \cdots \ge w_{(M)}(d_n)$ denote the posterior weights in decreasing order and define
\[
\gamma(d_n):=\log \frac{w_{(1)}(d_n)}{w_{(2)}(d_n)}.
\]
Then
\[
w_{(1)}(d_n)\ge \frac{1}{1+(M-1)e^{-\gamma(d_n)}}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let $\gamma=\log(w_{(1)}/w_{(2)})$. By definition, for every $j\ge 2$,
\[
w_{(j)}\le w_{(2)} = e^{-\gamma} w_{(1)}.
\]
Hence
\[
1=\sum_{j=1}^M w_{(j)}
\le
w_{(1)} + (M-1)e^{-\gamma}w_{(1)}
=
w_{(1)}\bigl(1+(M-1)e^{-\gamma}\bigr).
\]
Rearranging yields the claim. Combined with Lemma~\ref{lem:compression}, this gives the hard-selection penalty bound stated in the main text.
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:merge-stability}}
\label{app:proof-merge}

\begin{proof}
Define the quadratic surrogates
\[
Q_j(\theta):=c_j + \frac{1}{2}(\theta-\theta_j)^\top H(\theta-\theta_j),
\qquad
Q_w(\theta):=\sum_{j=1}^M w_j(d_n)Q_j(\theta).
\]
Because $\sum_j w_j(d_n)=1$, expanding the quadratic terms yields
\[
Q_w(\theta)
=
\mathrm{const}
+
\frac{1}{2}\left(\theta-\sum_{j=1}^M w_j(d_n)\theta_j\right)^\top
H
\left(\theta-\sum_{j=1}^M w_j(d_n)\theta_j\right).
\]
Therefore the minimizer of $Q_w$ over $U$ is
\[
\bar\theta_w(d_n):=\sum_{j=1}^M w_j(d_n)\theta_j.
\]
Since $|r_j(\theta)|\le \varepsilon_{\mathrm{quad}}$ for every $j$ and every $\theta\in U$, the weighted remainder also satisfies
\[
\left|L_{w(d_n)}(\theta)-Q_w(\theta)\right|
\le
\varepsilon_{\mathrm{quad}}
\qquad
\text{for all }\theta\in U.
\]
Hence
\[
L_{w(d_n)}(\theta_\alpha)-L_{w(d_n)}(\theta_w^\star)
\le
Q_w(\theta_\alpha)-Q_w(\theta_w^\star)+2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{quad}}
\le
Q_w(\theta_\alpha)-Q_w(\bar\theta_w)+2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{quad}}.
\]
The quadratic form for $Q_w$ gives
\[
Q_w(\theta_\alpha)-Q_w(\bar\theta_w)
=
\frac{1}{2}(\theta_\alpha-\bar\theta_w)^\top H(\theta_\alpha-\bar\theta_w)
\le
\frac{\lambda_{\max}(H)}{2}\|\theta_\alpha-\bar\theta_w\|^2.
\]
Finally,
\[
\theta_\alpha-\bar\theta_w
=
\sum_{j=1}^M (\alpha_j-w_j(d_n))\theta_j
=
\sum_{j=1}^M (\alpha_j-w_j(d_n))(\theta_j-\theta_0),
\]
because $\sum_j(\alpha_j-w_j(d_n))=0$. Using $\|\theta_j-\theta_0\|\le R$,
\[
\|\theta_\alpha-\bar\theta_w\|
\le
\sum_{j=1}^M |\alpha_j-w_j(d_n)|\,\|\theta_j-\theta_0\|
\le
R\|\alpha-w(d_n)\|_1.
\]
Substituting this into the previous display yields
\[
L_{w(d_n)}(\theta_\alpha)-L_{w(d_n)}(\theta_w^\star)
\le
\frac{\lambda_{\max}(H)}{2}R^2\|\alpha-w(d_n)\|_1^2+2\varepsilon_{\mathrm{quad}}.
\]
If $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{quad}}=0$ and $\alpha=w(d_n)$, then $\theta_\alpha=\bar\theta_w$ and the upper bound is zero, so the merge is an exact minimizer.
\end{proof}

\section{Fisher and Task-Vector Refinements}
\label{app:proof-fisher}

\begin{proposition}[Fisher/Hessian-weighted merge]
\label{prop:fisher-merge}
If the specialist objectives admit local quadratic approximations
\[
L_{\pi_j}(\theta)
\approx
c_j + \frac{1}{2}(\theta-\theta_j)^\top H_j(\theta-\theta_j),
\]
then the local minimizer of the support-adapted mixed objective is
\[
\theta^\star(d_n)
\approx
\left(\sum_{j=1}^M w_j(d_n)H_j\right)^{-1}
\left(\sum_{j=1}^M w_j(d_n)H_j\theta_j\right).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Substitute the quadratic expansions into $\sum_j w_j(d_n)L_{\pi_j}(\theta)$ and collect the terms that depend on $\theta$:
\[
\frac{1}{2}\theta^\top\left(\sum_j w_j H_j\right)\theta
-
\theta^\top\left(\sum_j w_j H_j\theta_j\right)
+ \text{const}.
\]
Differentiating with respect to $\theta$ and setting the gradient to zero gives
\[
\left(\sum_j w_j H_j\right)\theta
=
\sum_j w_j H_j\theta_j,
\]
which yields the stated expression whenever the weighted curvature matrix is invertible.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Weighted Task Vectors}
\label{app:proof-task}

\begin{proposition}[Weighted task vectors recover the first-order mixed update]
\label{prop:task-vector}
Suppose every specialist is obtained by fine-tuning a shared base checkpoint $\theta_0$ and that its displacement admits the decomposition
\[
\theta_j=\theta_0+\Delta_j,
\qquad
\Delta_j = -\eta P \nabla L_{\pi_j}(\theta_0) + r_j,
\]
for a common step size $\eta>0$, preconditioner $P$, and residual $r_j$. Then for every weight vector $\alpha\in\Delta_M$,
\[
\left\|
\sum_{j=1}^M \alpha_j \Delta_j
+
\eta P \nabla L_{\pi_\alpha}(\theta_0)
\right\|
\le
\sum_{j=1}^M \alpha_j \|r_j\|,
\qquad
L_{\pi_\alpha}:=\sum_{j=1}^M \alpha_j L_{\pi_j}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By linearity of the mixed objective,
\[
\nabla L_{\pi_\alpha}(\theta_0)=\sum_{j=1}^M \alpha_j \nabla L_{\pi_j}(\theta_0).
\]
Therefore
\[
\sum_j \alpha_j \Delta_j
=
-\eta P\sum_j \alpha_j \nabla L_{\pi_j}(\theta_0)
+
\sum_j \alpha_j r_j
=
-\eta P \nabla L_{\pi_\alpha}(\theta_0)
+
\sum_j \alpha_j r_j.
\]
Take norms and use the triangle inequality.
\end{proof}
